🔥 Warm-Up 0/4
✓
Stationary Bike 10 min · Easy Pace ▼
How to do it:
  1. Adjust the seat so your knee has a slight bend at the bottom of each pedal stroke.
  2. Set resistance to a light level — you should be able to hold a conversation.
  3. Sit upright with a straight spine; avoid hunching forward.
  4. Pedal at a comfortable, steady pace for 10 minutes.
Loosens lower back joints, increases blood flow to the discs, and prepares your spine for movement without compression.

✓
Cat-Cow Stretch 2 sets · 10 reps ▼
How to do it:
  1. Get on all fours — hands under shoulders, knees under hips.
  2. Cat: Exhale and round your spine up toward the ceiling, tucking your chin and pelvis.
  3. Cow: Inhale and drop your belly toward the floor, lifting your head and tailbone.
  4. Flow slowly between the two. Never force the range of motion.
One of the best mobilizers for arthritic lumbar joints. Move only as far as is pain-free.

✓
QL Ball Release 2–3 min · Each side ▼
How to do it:
  1. Place a tennis ball or lacrosse ball on the floor.
  2. Lie on your back and position the ball just above your hip bone, slightly to one side of your spine — that is your QL muscle (Quadratus Lumborum).
  3. Let your body weight slowly sink into the ball. Stay relaxed — do not tense up or fight it.
  4. When you find a sore or tight spot, pause there for 20–30 seconds, breathing slowly and deeply.
  5. Gently shift the ball to new spots along the muscle. Spend 2–3 minutes per side. More tender = more work needed there.
The QL muscle connects your pelvis to your spine and is a hidden driver of lumbar arthritis pain. Tight QLs cause your back muscles to overwork and your spine to compress unevenly. Releasing them before exercise reduces baseline tightness and makes your Bird Dog, Dead Bug, and Side Plank far more effective. Always release before stretching — not after.

✓
Deep Core Activation 2 sets · 10 reps ▼
How to do it:
  1. Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat on the floor. Place your fingertips just inside your hip bones (the bony bumps at the front of your pelvis).
  2. Take a slow breath in through your nose.
  3. As you exhale slowly, gently draw your lower abdomen inward — as if bracing for a light poke in the stomach. Do NOT suck your stomach in hard or hold your breath.
  4. You should feel a subtle, deep tightening under your fingertips. That is your transverse abdominis — your deep core "corset" muscle — switching on.
  5. Hold the gentle brace for 5–6 seconds while continuing to breathe normally. Release fully. That is one rep. Do 10 reps.
Most back pain sufferers have a "turned off" deep core after months of guarding. This exercise wakes it back up. Your Bird Dog and Dead Bug only work properly if this foundation is already activated. Takes 2 minutes and pays dividends the entire workout. Focus on the internal sensation — this is quiet, internal work, not a visible movement.

🧘 Mobility & Stretching 0/3
✓
Knee-to-Chest Stretch 30 sec · Each leg ▼
How to do it:
  1. Lie on your back, knees bent.
  2. Slowly pull one knee toward your chest, clasping your hands behind the thigh.
  3. Hold 30 seconds — feel a gentle stretch in the lower back and glute.
  4. Switch legs. Then pull both knees to chest for 30 more seconds.
Gently opens the lumbar facet joints where arthritis and bone spurs develop, reducing morning stiffness.

✓
Figure-4 Piriformis Stretch 30 sec · Each side ▼
How to do it:
  1. Lie on your back, knees bent.
  2. Cross your right ankle over your left knee (figure-4 shape).
  3. Reach through and pull your left thigh toward your chest until you feel a stretch in the right glute/hip.
  4. Hold 30 seconds, switch sides.
A tight piriformis can compress the sciatic nerve and worsen back pain. Often one of the most immediately relieving stretches.

✓
Hip Flexor Stretch (Kneeling) 30 sec · Each side ▼
How to do it:
  1. Kneel on one knee on a mat (folded towel under knee if needed).
  2. Place other foot flat on floor in front, making a 90° angle.
  3. Push hips forward gently until you feel a stretch in the front of the back hip.
  4. Hold 30 seconds. Keep spine upright — don't arch your lower back. Switch sides.
Tight hip flexors pull the pelvis forward and increase lumbar compression. Stretching them daily is one of the highest-impact things you can do.

💪 Core & Stability 0/5
✓
Bird Dog 3 sets · 10 reps each side ▼
How to do it:
  1. Start on all fours — hands under shoulders, knees under hips.
  2. Brace your core gently (like you're about to be lightly poked in the stomach).
  3. Slowly extend your right arm forward and left leg back simultaneously.
  4. Hold 2–3 seconds. Keep hips level — don't rotate.
  5. Return slowly, repeat on opposite side. That's one rep.
Trains the muscles directly along your spine without loading it. One of the top physical therapy exercises for lumbar issues.
▶ Watch Video
✓
Dead Bug 3 sets · 8 reps each side ▼
How to do it:
  1. Lie on your back. Lift both arms straight up. Lift both knees to 90°.
  2. Press your lower back firmly into the floor — keep it there throughout.
  3. Slowly lower right arm overhead and extend left leg toward floor simultaneously.
  4. Stop before your back arches. Return to start. Repeat other side.
Trains your core to resist movement — exactly what it needs to protect your lumbar spine. Go only as far as your back stays flat.

✓
Glute Bridge 3 sets · 15 reps ▼
How to do it:
  1. Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat hip-width apart.
  2. Press through your heels and squeeze glutes to lift hips off the floor.
  3. Body forms a straight line from shoulders to knees. Do NOT hyperextend lower back.
  4. Hold 2 seconds at top, then slowly lower. Breathe normally.
Weak glutes are a #1 cause of back pain — they make your back muscles overcompensate. Strong glutes = less load on your lumbar spine.

✓
Side Plank 2–3 sets · 20–40 sec · Each side ▼
How to do it:
  1. Lie on your right side on a mat. Stack your feet or stagger them for balance.
  2. Place your right forearm on the floor, elbow directly under your shoulder.
  3. Brace your core gently, then lift your hips so your body forms a straight line from head to heels. Squeeze your glutes.
  4. Hold 20–40 seconds, breathing normally. Do not let your hips sag or pike up.
  5. Lower slowly with control. Switch to the left side and repeat.
  6. Easier version (start here): Keep your bottom knee on the floor — same line, just a shorter lever. Much more manageable for beginners.
The Side Plank directly strengthens the QL muscle — the same one you released with the ball in warm-up. It builds lateral stability without compressing, flexing, or rotating the spine, which is critical for lumbar arthritis. Start on your knees and work up to the full version over several weeks. William Richards (Fitness 4 Back Pain) specifically recommends this for arthritis and disc patients.

✓
Paloff Press Phase 3+ · 2–3 sets · 10–12 reps · Each side ▼
How to do it:
  1. At a cable machine, set a D-handle at chest height.
  2. Stand sideways to the machine, feet shoulder-width apart. Grab the handle with both hands and hold it at your chest, close to your sternum.
  3. Step far enough away from the machine that there is light tension on the cable.
  4. Brace your deep core (like you practiced in Deep Core Activation). Keep your hips square — do not let the cable pull you sideways or rotate your torso.
  5. Slowly press your hands straight out in front of you until your arms are fully extended. Pause 1–2 seconds — feel your core resisting the rotation.
  6. Slowly return your hands to your chest. That is one rep. Complete all reps on one side, then turn around and repeat facing the other direction.
The Paloff Press trains your core to resist rotation — completely different from planks and dead bugs. This protects your lumbar spine during everyday twisting (turning to reverse a car, reaching sideways, getting out of bed). You are already at the cable machine for Seated Cable Row, so this adds only 5–6 minutes. Introduced in Phase 3 once your deep core foundation is established. Start very light — the challenge is staying perfectly still, not the weight.

🦵 Lower Body Strength (Spine-Friendly) 0/4
✓
Leg Press Machine 3 sets · 12 reps · Light weight ▼
How to do it:
  1. Sit in the machine with back fully supported against the pad.
  2. Place feet shoulder-width apart on the platform.
  3. Lower platform until knees are around 90° — don't let knees cave inward.
  4. Push through heels to extend (don't lock out knees at the top).
  5. Start with very light weight — not a max-effort exercise.
Builds quads, glutes, and hamstrings WITHOUT loading your spine — unlike squats or deadlifts. The safest lower body machine for back conditions.

✓
Hip Abductor Machine 3 sets · 15 reps · Light weight ▼
How to do it:
  1. Sit in the machine with pads on the outside of your knees.
  2. Sit tall, back against pad, core lightly braced.
  3. Press knees outward against pads in a controlled motion.
  4. Slowly return — don't let the weight slam back.
Hip abductors stabilize your pelvis. Weak abductors cause the pelvis to drop when walking, transferring stress directly to the lower back.

✓
Hip Adductor Machine 3 sets · 15 reps · Light weight ▼
How to do it:
  1. Sit in the adductor machine with pads on the inside of your knees.
  2. Sit upright, back against pad, feet on footrests.
  3. Squeeze knees inward together against the pads in a controlled motion.
  4. Slowly return. Keep core gently engaged throughout.
Inner thigh strength balances the hip abductors and keeps the pelvis properly aligned, reducing asymmetric loading on your lumbar spine.

✓
Seated Hamstring Curl Machine 3 sets · 12 reps · Light weight ▼
How to do it:
  1. Sit in the machine so the back of your ankles rest on the lower pad.
  2. Sit tall with back against the support pad.
  3. Curl your legs downward by bending at the knee.
  4. Squeeze at the bottom, then slowly return. Don't let the weight drop.
Tight, weak hamstrings rotate the pelvis and increase disc and facet joint stress. The seated version is spine-neutral unlike the lying version.

🏋️ Upper Body (Spinal Decompression) 0/2
✓
Lat Pulldown 3 sets · 12 reps · Moderate weight ▼
How to do it:
  1. Sit at the lat pulldown machine, thighs secured under pads.
  2. Grip bar slightly wider than shoulder-width, palms facing away.
  3. Lean back slightly (~15°) and pull bar down to upper chest.
  4. Squeeze shoulder blades together at bottom. Slowly return to full arm extension.
  5. Do NOT yank or use momentum — slow and controlled.
Pulling exercises gently decompress the lumbar spine while building upper back muscles that support posture — a double benefit for back pain sufferers.
▶ Watch Video
✓
Seated Cable Row 3 sets · 12 reps · Moderate weight ▼
How to do it:
  1. Sit at cable row station with feet on footrests, knees slightly bent.
  2. Sit tall — back upright, chest forward. Grip the handle.
  3. Pull handle toward lower chest/belly button while squeezing shoulder blades together.
  4. Hold 1 second. Slowly return to full arm extension without rounding your back.
  5. Avoid rocking forward and back to generate momentum.
Strengthens the mid-back which pulls shoulders back and reduces the forward-rounding posture that compresses lumbar discs all day.

❄️ Cool-Down 0/2
✓
Seated Hamstring Stretch 30 sec · Each leg ▼
How to do it:
  1. Sit on the edge of a bench. Extend one leg straight in front.
  2. Keeping back straight (don't round!), hinge forward at the hips until you feel a stretch in the back of the thigh.
  3. Hold 30 seconds. Breathe deeply. Switch legs.
  4. If too intense, bend the knee slightly.
Stretching warm hamstrings after exercise is far more effective than cold stretching. Pays off in reduced back tightness the next day.
▶ Watch Video
✓
Child's Pose 2 sets · 60 sec ▼
How to do it:
  1. Kneel on a mat, big toes together, knees apart.
  2. Sit hips back toward heels as far as comfortably possible.
  3. Reach arms forward on the floor, let forehead rest on the mat.
  4. Breathe deeply into your lower back. Feel the gentle stretch release with each breath.
  5. If knees are uncomfortable, place a folded towel under them.
Gently decompresses the lumbar vertebrae. One of the most universally relieving finishers for lower back conditions. Never skip this one.
